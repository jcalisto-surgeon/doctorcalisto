When a second opinion makes sense

If your child continues to have soiling, severe constipation, recurrent obstruction, enterocolitis, or anatomy that doesn't seem right after a pull-through, anorectal reconstruction, or other colorectal surgery, those problems deserve a careful second look. They are often fixable.

A structured re-evaluation can identify what's actually happening — and whether reoperative (redo) surgery, a revision, or a tailored bowel-management plan would help. The goal is a child who is clean, comfortable, and confident. That is exactly the right time to ask for another opinion.

  • Complex and redo anorectal and pelvic reconstruction

Frequently asked questions

When should I seek a second opinion?
If your child still has soiling, constipation, recurrent obstruction, enterocolitis, or symptoms that don't add up after colorectal surgery, a second opinion is reasonable — and often the right move. An early conversation frequently changes the plan for the better.
What happens at a re-evaluation?
Dr. Calisto reviews your child's history, prior operative notes, and imaging, and examines your child to map the current anatomy and function. From there he explains what's happening in plain language and what the realistic options are — which may or may not include further surgery.
Does a second opinion mean my child needs another operation?
Not necessarily. Sometimes the answer is a tailored bowel-management program rather than surgery. The point of the evaluation is to find the least intervention that gets your child clean, comfortable, and growing.
My child was treated in another country — can you still help?
Yes. Dr. Calisto regularly cares for children referred from across the U.S. and Latin America, and also leads charitable surgical care abroad through Red de Corazones. Reach out and his team will guide you on next steps.
